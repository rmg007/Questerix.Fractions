# PEDAGOGICAL PHILOSOPHY

## Why We Teach Grade 1 Fractions This Way

## INTRODUCTION

Every choice in this curriculum is deliberate. Every activity is grounded in
research about how 6–7-year-old children actually learn mathematics. This
document explains the _why_ behind the _what_.

If you are a teacher, designer, or skeptic who wants to know whether this
curriculum is rigorous, this is the document for you.

## I. THE LEARNER WE ARE DESIGNING FOR

### Cognitive Profile of a Typical Grade 1 Student (Age 6–7)

```
COGNITIVE READINESS (Piaget's Concrete Operational Stage, beginning)
├─ Can perform mental operations on CONCRETE objects but not abstractions
├─ Conservation (understanding that quantity persists despite shape change)
│   is JUST EMERGING — about 60–70% of 6-year-olds have it stable
├─ Reversibility (mental "undoing") is fragile; needs scaffolding
├─ Decentration (considering multiple attributes at once) is partial
└─ Egocentrism in language is decreasing but still present

WORKING MEMORY
├─ Capacity: 3 ± 1 chunks (vs. adult 7 ± 2)
├─ Implication: Each activity must isolate ONE concept
├─ Multitasking is impossible; one task at a time
└─ Verbal instructions > 2 steps cause overload

ATTENTION SPAN
├─ Sustained focused attention: 8–15 minutes
├─ With novelty/movement: up to 25 minutes
├─ Implication: Activity blocks must be short, varied
└─ Transitions must be explicit and predictable

LANGUAGE
├─ Vocabulary: 2,500–6,000 words (huge variation by SES)
├─ Reading: Most are decoding-focused, not yet fluent
├─ Listening comprehension > reading comprehension
├─ Implication: Auditory + visual instructions, minimal text
└─ Math vocabulary must be EXPLICITLY taught (not assumed)

MOTOR SKILLS
├─ Fine motor: Still developing; scissors are work
├─ Gross motor: Strong; benefit from movement
├─ Implication: Activities should leverage gross over fine when possible
└─ Tool use (rulers, etc.) takes longer than for adults

SOCIAL-EMOTIONAL
├─ Strong identification with teacher (please-the-teacher motivation)
├─ Peer relationships intensifying; comparison sensitivity high
├─ Self-concept as "smart" or "dumb" forming RIGHT NOW
├─ Math anxiety can begin in Grade 1 if poorly handled (Beilock, 2010)
└─ Identity-safe environments are critical
```

### Implication for Curriculum Design

A curriculum that ignores this profile (which is most curricula) will:

- Overwhelm working memory
- Force premature abstraction
- Trigger math anxiety
- Create "I'm bad at math" identity narratives

Our curriculum is designed to fit this profile, not fight it.

## II. THE SIX FOUNDATIONAL FRAMEWORKS

### Framework 1: Bruner's CRA Sequence

**Source**: Bruner, J. (1966). _Toward a Theory of Instruction._

**Principle**: Children learn mathematics in three modes, in this order:

- **Concrete (Enactive)**: Physical objects they manipulate.
- **Representational (Iconic)**: Pictures, drawings, models.
- **Abstract (Symbolic)**: Numerals, equations, formal symbols.

**Standard Mistake**: Most curricula skip from concrete (briefly) to abstract,
hoping representational will fill in. It doesn't.

**Our Application**:

```
PHASE 1 (6 weeks): 100% CONCRETE
├─ Real food, real bodies, real paper, real blocks
├─ NO drawings of fractions
├─ NO symbols (1/2, 1/4)
└─ NO digital simulations

PHASE 2 (14 weeks): 70% CONCRETE / 25% REPRESENTATIONAL / 5% SYMBOLIC
├─ Continued physical activities
├─ Introduction of pie charts, bar models (representational)
├─ Symbol "1/2" introduced WEEK 14 only after representations are stable
└─ Symbol "1/4" introduced WEEK 18

PHASE 3 (12 weeks): 30% CONCRETE / 50% REPRESENTATIONAL / 20% SYMBOLIC
├─ Concrete remains for new concepts (e.g., fourths via decomposition)
├─ Representational becomes primary mode
├─ Symbols increasingly used in word problems
└─ But concrete is ALWAYS available for any child
```

**Evidence**: Witzel, Mercer & Miller (2003) found CRA increased fraction
mastery in struggling students by 40% over symbolic-first instruction.

---

### Framework 2: Piaget's Concrete Operational Stage

**Source**: Piaget, J. (1952). _The Child's Conception of Number._

**Principle**: Children aged 6–11 reason logically but ONLY about concrete
objects. They cannot yet do operations on operations (formal abstract reasoning).

**Specific Implication for Fractions**:

- A child who has not achieved "conservation" cannot understand that two halves
  equal one whole, even if they can recite it.
- Conservation of liquid (Piaget's classic experiment) typically arrives 6–7.
- Conservation of fractions arrives later — sometimes Grade 2 or 3.

**Our Application**:

```
WE DO NOT TEACH "1/2 + 1/2 = 1" ABSTRACTLY IN GRADE 1.

Instead, we teach:
"When Halfie and Halvo come together, they make Whola."
"When you put your two halves of paper together, they make the whole."

The CONCRETE physical reunification builds the schema. The abstract equation
comes in Grade 3, when conservation is stable.

We test (informally) for conservation:
- Show child a whole cookie. Cut it in half.
- Ask: "Is this still as much cookie as before, or more, or less?"
- If child says "less," they have NOT conserved. They need more concrete work.
- If child says "the same," they have conserved. They can advance.
```

**Why This Matters**: Forcing abstract instruction on children who haven't
conserved produces ROTE LEARNING that collapses under pressure. The child can
parrot "1/2 + 1/2 = 1" but cannot apply it to a real cookie.

### Framework 3: Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)

**Source**: Vygotsky, L. (1978). _Mind in Society._

**Principle**: Children learn most in the gap between what they can do alone
(actual development) and what they can do with help (potential development).

**Implication**: Tasks should be challenging but achievable with scaffolding.
Too easy = bored. Too hard = frustrated. Just right (ZPD) = engaged.

**Our Application**:

```
EVERY ACTIVITY HAS THREE ENTRY POINTS:

1. INDEPENDENT (already mastered) — for child who has prior knowledge
2. SCAFFOLDED (in ZPD) — for child who needs guidance
3. INTENSIVE (below ZPD) — for child needing pre-requisite work

EXAMPLE: Activity 5 (Pizza Party Protocol)

Independent: "Cut your pizza into halves. Now make fourths."
Scaffolded: "Here's your pizza. Fold it once — what do you have?
            Fold it again — what do you have now?"
Intensive: "Pia has one pizza. She has two friends. Show me how to share."
           [Reverts to Phase 1 sharing concepts.]

Teacher decides which entry point per child based on observation.
```

**Critical**: ZPD is NOT a fixed property of the child. The same child may need
intensive support for halves and independent for sharing. Differentiation is
not a label — it's responsive to the moment.

---

### Framework 4: Embodied Cognition

**Source**: Lakoff & Núñez (2000). _Where Mathematics Comes From._

**Principle**: Mathematical concepts are NOT abstractions floating in mind;
they are grounded in bodily experience. Counting comes from finger-counting.
"Greater than" comes from physical largeness. Halves come from bodily division.

**Specific to Fractions**:

- "Half" originates in bodily symmetry (left/right, top/bottom)
- "Equal" originates in bodily fairness (you got the same as me)
- "Whole" originates in unity of self (one body)

**Our Application**:

```
ACTIVITY 3: BODY MATH — HALVES

Children physically experience halving:
- "Find the halfway point of your arm" (touch elbow)
- "Find the halfway point of your body" (touch belly button)
- "Stand together with a friend; you are halves of the pair"
- "Fold your body forward — now your top is one half, bottom is the other"

This builds an EMBODIED SCHEMA for halving that becomes the foundation for all
later abstract reasoning. When a child later sees "1/2" on paper, their brain
activates this bodily schema.

Children with strong bodily schemas for math have higher long-term retention
and better transfer (Glenberg, 2008; Tran et al., 2017).
```

---

### Framework 5: Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

**Source**: CAST (2018). _Universal Design for Learning Guidelines._

**Principle**: Design curriculum so it works for the widest range of learners
from the start, rather than retrofitting accommodations.

**Three UDL Networks**:

```
1. RECOGNITION NETWORKS (the "what" of learning)
   → Multiple means of representation

   Our application:
   ├─ Every concept presented in 3+ ways:
   │   ├─ Visual (drawings, models)
   │   ├─ Auditory (story, teacher narration, songs)
   │   ├─ Tactile (folding, manipulating)
   │   └─ Kinesthetic (body math)
   └─ ELL support: visual + cognate vocabulary

2. STRATEGIC NETWORKS (the "how" of learning)
   → Multiple means of action and expression

   Our application:
   ├─ Children can demonstrate understanding by:
   │   ├─ Drawing
   │   ├─ Folding
   │   ├─ Talking
   │   ├─ Building (blocks)
   │   ├─ Acting out (drama)
   │   └─ Singing
   └─ No single "right" way to show mastery

3. AFFECTIVE NETWORKS (the "why" of learning)
   → Multiple means of engagement

   Our application:
   ├─ Story narrative provides intrinsic motivation
   ├─ Choice within activities (which shape to fold)
   ├─ Social learning (paired sharing)
   ├─ Authentic contexts (real food, real situations)
   └─ Identity-safe environment
```

---

### Framework 6: Cognitive Load Theory

**Source**: Sweller, J. (2011). _Cognitive Load Theory._

**Principle**: Working memory has limited capacity. Curriculum must minimize
EXTRANEOUS cognitive load (anything not directly related to the learning goal)
to free capacity for INTRINSIC and GERMANE load (the actual learning).

**Common Mistakes That Overload**:

- Cluttered worksheets with decorative borders, multiple problem types
- Instructions in long paragraphs
- Multiple objectives per lesson
- Switching between contexts (paper, then digital, then verbal)

**Our Application**:

```
EACH ACTIVITY ISOLATES ONE NEW CONCEPT.

✗ BAD: "Today we'll learn halves AND fourths AND sharing AND vocabulary."
✓ GOOD: "Today we'll learn ONE thing: what makes shares EQUAL."

Activity 1: Just "wholeness"
Activity 2: Just "equal vs. unequal"
Activity 3: Just halves (no fourths yet)
... and so on.

Materials are MINIMAL.
- Each activity uses 1–3 types of objects
- No extraneous decoration
- Instructions are spoken in 1–2 sentences

Visual displays are CLEAN.
- Whiteboard has only today's concept
- Anchor charts are simple, visual
- Math word wall is uncluttered
```

**Evidence**: Sweller, Ayres & Kalyuga (2011) showed that worked-example
problems with low extraneous load improved transfer by 30% in mathematics.

## III. THE THREE PILLARS REVISITED — WITH EVIDENCE

### Pillar 1: WHOLENESS

**Common Curriculum Failure**: Skipping the concept of "whole" entirely. Most
curricula start with "here's half of a circle" without ever teaching what a
"whole" is.

**Why This Fails**: Without a schema for "whole," fractions become arbitrary
shapes. The child doesn't understand that 1/2 means "one of two equal parts of
a unit" because they have no schema for "unit."

**Evidence**: Empson & Levi (2011) found that 40% of struggling fraction
students (Grades 3–5) lacked a stable concept of the "unit," tracing back to
weak Grade 1–2 instruction.

**Our Approach**:

```
WEEK 1–2: ACTIVITY 1 — WHOLE DISCOVERY

Children explore:
- "What makes a pizza WHOLE?" (it's all there, none missing)
- "What makes a cookie WHOLE?" (same)
- "What if I take a bite? Is it still whole?" (NO — it's a part)
- "What if I put the bite back?" (this question is GENIUS for 6-year-olds)

Children sort objects:
WHOLE pile: complete pizza, complete apple, complete book, complete crayon
NOT WHOLE pile: bitten apple, broken crayon, missing puzzle piece

This is unfamiliar territory for 6-year-olds — they have intuitive sense but
not explicit language. Making it explicit BUILDS THE SCHEMA.

ASSESSMENT: Child sorts 10 objects with 9/10 accuracy = mastered.
```

---

### Pillar 2: EQUALITY

**Common Curriculum Failure**: Pre-dividing shapes into equal parts, removing
the child's opportunity to discover what "equal" means.

**Why This Fails**: When the curriculum always shows pre-divided equal halves,
the child develops the misconception that ANY two-part division = halves. When
they see an unequal split, they label it "halves" anyway.

**Evidence**: Wong & Evans (2007) found that 60% of Grade 2 students (post
fraction instruction) accepted unequal partitions as "halves" because their
Grade 1 curriculum never explicitly contrasted equal and unequal.

**Our Approach**:

```
WEEK 3–4: ACTIVITY 2 — EQUAL OR NOT?

Children sort sharing scenarios:
- Two children, two cookies (one big, one small) — UNEQUAL
- Two children, two cookies (same size) — EQUAL
- Two children, one cookie cut down the middle — EQUAL HALVES
- Two children, one cookie with one tiny sliver — UNEQUAL

We use the word "FAIR" before "EQUAL" because fair is in 6-year-old vocabulary.
Then we BRIDGE: "Fair shares are EQUAL shares."

ASSESSMENT: Child identifies "fair" vs "unfair" sharing in 9/10 trials.
```

---

### Pillar 3: REUNIFICATION

**Common Curriculum Failure**: Teaching only the forward direction (whole →
parts). Not teaching the reverse (parts → whole).

**Why This Fails**: Without reversibility, the child doesn't understand the
RELATIONSHIP between part and whole — only the procedure of dividing.

**Evidence**: Confrey (1994) demonstrated that "splitting" (whole → parts) and
"unitizing" (parts → whole) are both required for true fraction understanding,
and that most curricula neglect unitizing.

**Our Approach**:

```
WEEK 24–25: ACTIVITY 11 — BUILD A WHOLE

Children are GIVEN parts and asked to construct wholes:
- "Here are two halves of a pizza. Make them whole."
- "Here are four fourths of a sandwich. Make them whole."
- "Here is one half. Where's the other half?"
- "Here are three fourths. Are they whole?" (NO — missing one fourth)

This builds REVERSIBILITY of the fraction operation, which is conceptually
critical and almost universally neglected in Grade 1 curricula.

ASSESSMENT: Child reassembles wholes from parts in 5/5 trials.
```

## IV. EQUITY-CENTERED PEDAGOGY

### The Stakes in Grade 1

Math identity ("I'm a math person" or "I'm not") begins to crystallize in
Grade 1 (Cvencek, Meltzoff, & Greenwald, 2011). Children who feel mathematically
incompetent in Grade 1 are 5x more likely to disengage from math by Grade 5.

**This is especially true for**:

- Girls (despite no actual ability difference)
- Black and Latinx children (due to stereotype threat exposure)
- Children from low-income backgrounds (due to less prior exposure to math
  vocabulary)
- Multilingual learners (due to language demands of math)

**Our curriculum is designed to PROTECT and BUILD math identity for all.**

### Specific Anti-Inequity Design Choices

**1. No Public Comparison**

- No leaderboards
- No "fastest" recognition
- No "smartest" labels
- Mastery is internal, not relative

**2. Multiple Entry Points**

- Children can be brilliant at body math but slow at folding (and vice versa)
- Multiple modes mean every child can shine somewhere

**3. Identity-Safe Language**

- Teacher addresses children as "Mathematicians"
- "When you struggle, your brain is GROWING"
- Mistakes are framed as essential to learning (Boaler, 2016)

**4. Asset-Based Framing**

- ELL students bring linguistic richness ("mitad" → "half")
- Low-income students bring resourcefulness (sharing experience)
- Children with disabilities bring diverse perspectives
- All assets are explicitly named and valued

**5. Family Funds of Knowledge**

- Sharing scenarios reflect home contexts (tortilla, naan, dosa, pizza)
- Parent companion document invites family math stories
- Multilingual home activities

**6. Anti-Bias Vocabulary**

- "Boys and girls" → "Mathematicians" or "Friends"
- Pronoun-flexible characters (Pia is gender-flexibly named)
- Diverse representation in all visuals

**Evidence**: Aguirre, Mayfield-Ingram, & Martin (2013) demonstrated that
identity-safe, asset-based math instruction in K–2 closes achievement gaps by
50% by Grade 5.

## V. WHAT WE DO NOT DO (AND WHY)

### We Do Not Use Worksheets (Except Sparingly)

**Reasoning**:

- Worksheets are typically 80% extraneous load (decorative, motor demands)
  and only 20% concept.
- Children can complete worksheets without understanding (pattern matching).
- Worksheets prioritize speed and fine motor over thinking.

**Exception**: We use 4–5 highly-curated worksheets all year, as evidence in
portfolios. They are NOT instructional tools.

---

### We Do Not Time Children

**Reasoning**:

- Math anxiety begins in Grade 1 with timed instruction (Boaler, 2014).
- Speed is not a measure of understanding.
- Slow processors are often deeper thinkers.

**No Exception**: There is no scenario in this curriculum where a Grade 1
child is timed.

---

### We Do Not Introduce Symbols Early

**Reasoning**:

- Symbol-first instruction creates shallow learning (rote recitation).
- The symbol "1/2" is meaningless without the concrete schema.
- Premature symbols crowd out conceptual development.

**Exception**: After Week 14 (Phase 2, mid-year), we informally introduce "1/2"
as "what we WRITE for 'one half'" — but never assess on it in Grade 1.

---

### We Do Not Use Pre-Divided Models Exclusively

**Reasoning**:

- Children must DO the dividing themselves to build schema.
- Pre-divided models hide the partitioning operation.

**Application**: Children fold their OWN papers. They cut their OWN cookies.
They draw their OWN dividing lines.

---

### We Do Not Compare Fractions in Grade 1

**Reasoning**:

- Comparing fractions (1/2 vs 1/4) requires understanding both fractions
  AND the relationship between them. This is double cognitive load.
- Phase 2 builds individual fraction concepts deeply.
- Comparison is the GRADE 2 standard (CCSS 2.G.A.3 and ramps to 3.NF).

**Exception**: We INFORMALLY note that "fourths are smaller than halves" but
this is descriptive, not the focus of instruction.

## VI. SCAFFOLDING ARCHITECTURE

Every activity has a deliberate scaffolding structure:

```
LEVEL 1: TEACHER MODELS (I do)
├─ Teacher demonstrates with all language
├─ Children observe and listen
└─ Duration: 1–2 minutes

LEVEL 2: GUIDED PRACTICE (We do)
├─ Teacher and children do together
├─ Teacher prompts with language
├─ Children repeat language
└─ Duration: 3–5 minutes

LEVEL 3: COLLABORATIVE PRACTICE (You do together)
├─ Children work in pairs or small groups
├─ Teacher circulates and supports
├─ Peer language exchange
└─ Duration: 5–10 minutes

LEVEL 4: INDEPENDENT PRACTICE (You do)
├─ Children work alone
├─ Teacher observes and notes
├─ Children self-monitor
└─ Duration: 3–5 minutes
```

**The Gradual Release of Responsibility (Pearson & Gallagher, 1983)** is the
research-backed structure for scaffolded learning. It ensures children move
from external support to internal mastery.

## VII. ASSESSMENT PHILOSOPHY (DEEPER TREATMENT)

### What Assessment Is FOR (in Grade 1)

Assessment in Grade 1 should:

1. **Inform teaching** (formative assessment)
2. **Document growth** (portfolio assessment)
3. **Identify struggles** (diagnostic assessment)
4. **Celebrate mastery** (affirming assessment)

Assessment in Grade 1 should NOT:

1. ❌ Sort children into ability groups
2. ❌ Generate grades or percentages
3. ❌ Rank or compare children
4. ❌ Identify "smart" or "not smart"

### Three Assessment Modes (Continuous)

```
1. OBSERVATIONAL ASSESSMENT (DAILY)
   Teacher uses simple rubric while children work:
   ├─ ✗ Not yet attempting
   ├─ → Attempting with support
   ├─ ✓ Attempting independently
   └─ ★ Confident and explaining

   No quizzes. No tests. Just watching with intention.

2. CONFERRING (WEEKLY, 5 MIN PER CHILD)
   Teacher sits with one child:
   ├─ "Show me halves with this paper."
   ├─ "What's a half?"
   ├─ "Why are fourths smaller than halves?"
   └─ Notes child's exact words; identifies misconceptions.

   This is the most powerful assessment for understanding cognition.

3. PERFORMANCE TASKS (PHASE GATES, 3X YEAR)
   At end of each phase, child does a 10-min performance task:
   ├─ Concrete + verbal demonstration of mastery
   ├─ Recorded by teacher (notes or video)
   └─ Used to determine readiness for next phase.
```

### Documentation: The Portfolio

Each child has a portfolio containing:

- Photographs of their folded paper, constructions
- Drawings they made
- Quotes from conferring (what they SAID)
- Teacher observation notes
- Phase-gate performance task evidence

This portfolio is:

- Shared with parents quarterly
- Sent home at end of year
- Foundation for Grade 2 teacher's understanding

**Why Portfolios?** They capture LEARNING TRAJECTORY, not just snapshots.
They show growth, struggle, breakthrough — the actual story of learning.
A grade can never do this.

## VIII. THE ROLE OF PLAY

> "Play is the highest form of research." — attributed to Einstein

Grade 1 children play. Always. The question isn't whether to use play, but how
to harness it.

### Play in This Curriculum

**Structured Play** (most activities): Activities have clear goals but feel
playful. Folding paper to make halves IS play. Sharing pizza IS play.

**Free Play with Math Materials** (15 min weekly): Children have access to
manipulatives (pattern blocks, fraction circles, paper) and can explore freely.
Teacher observes for emergent understanding.

**Imaginative Play** (story-driven activities): Children become characters
(Halfie, Halvo). Drama supports cognitive development.

**Game Play** (digital activities, 5 min): Brief digital reinforcement that
feels game-like, but is pedagogically targeted.

**Why Play Matters**:

- Reduces cognitive anxiety (allows risk-taking)
- Increases engagement (intrinsic motivation)
- Builds creative problem-solving
- Supports embodied schema formation

(Vygotsky, 1978; Hirsh-Pasek et al., 2009)

## IX. THE TEACHER AS ARCHITECT

This curriculum is NOT teacher-proof. It is teacher-empowering.

### What the Teacher Does

```
PLANNING (5–10 min/day)
├─ Review tomorrow's activity card
├─ Gather materials
├─ Anticipate student misconceptions
└─ Plan differentiation

INSTRUCTION (15–25 min/day)
├─ Tell story segment
├─ Lead concrete experience
├─ Facilitate reflection
├─ Coach digital reinforcement (if used)
├─ Run Crumb Check
└─ Close with preview

OBSERVATION (Throughout)
├─ Use rubric to track each child
├─ Note breakthroughs and struggles
├─ Identify misconceptions
└─ Plan next-day differentiation

CONFERRING (15 min/day, 1:1 with 3 children)
├─ Sit with child, ask 2–3 questions
├─ Document responses
├─ Identify next instructional move
└─ Build relationship with child

REFLECTION (5 min weekly)
├─ Review observation data
├─ Plan small-group needs
└─ Update parent communication
```

**Total teacher time**: ~40 min/day for fractions block + ~30 min planning/week.
This fits realistic teacher schedules.

### What the Teacher Does NOT Do

- ❌ Grade worksheets (we don't use them)
- ❌ Generate spreadsheets of percentages
- ❌ Give pop quizzes
- ❌ Manage complex software dashboards
- ❌ Create elaborate visuals (we provide them)

## XI. IF YOU REMEMBER NOTHING ELSE

If you implement this curriculum and forget every theoretical justification,
remember these five principles:

1. **CONCRETE BEFORE ABSTRACT.** Always.
2. **EQUAL VS. UNEQUAL.** Make this discrimination explicit.
3. **WHOLENESS.** Teach what "one" is before what "half" is.
4. **REUNIFICATION.** Parts make wholes; teach both directions.
5. **NO PRESSURE.** No timing, no comparison, no shame.
