# GRADE 1 FRACTION MISCONCEPTIONS FRAMEWORK

> *"A misconception is not a failure of intelligence. It is a reasonable interpretation of incomplete data. The job of the curriculum is to make the data complete — and to honor the reasoning that led to the misconception."*

---

## WHY A FRAMEWORK?

Misconceptions in Grade 1 fraction learning are **predictable, patterned, and persistent** across classrooms, cultures, and decades of instruction (Behr et al., 1983; Empson & Levi, 2011; Lamon, 2012).

A misconception is not:
- A child being "wrong"
- A sign of low ability
- A random error

A misconception IS:
- A child's best current theory about how the world works
- A coherent interpretation of limited evidence
- A predictable stop on the learning trajectory

**Curriculum design principle:** Anticipate misconceptions. Address them through carefully chosen examples, non-examples, and explicit conversations. Never assume they'll "go away naturally" — without attention, they persist into adulthood.

---

## THE M1-M12 TAXONOMY

We identify **12 major misconceptions** in Grade 1 fraction learning. Each is assigned a code (M1-M12) for reference across curriculum documents.

---

### M1 — Size ≠ Wholeness

**The Misconception:** "Bigger things are more whole than smaller things."

**Evidence it's happening:**
- Child identifies large objects as "wholes" and small ones as "parts"
- Resists recognizing a tiny apple as a "whole apple"

**Where addressed:**
- Week 1, Day 3 (Different Shapes, Same Whole)
- Recurring: use deliberately varied-size examples

**Resolution language:**
> *"A whole is ONE COMPLETE thing. A small pizza is a whole. A big pizza is a whole. Both are WHOLES."*

---

### M2 — Whole = Circle

**The Misconception:** "Only circles are wholes (or can be halved/fourthed)."

**Evidence:**
- Child halves circles confidently but balks at rectangles
- Insists halves "should" look like semicircles

**Where addressed:**
- Week 1, Day 3 (multiple shapes introduced)
- Weeks 7-9 (folding diverse shapes)

**Resolution language:**
> *"Wholes come in MANY shapes. So do halves. A half of a rectangle looks different from a half of a circle. Both are halves."*

---

### M3 — Any Two Pieces = Halves

**The Misconception:** "If I cut something into two pieces, those are halves."

**Evidence:**
- Child produces unequal pieces and calls them halves
- Doesn't self-check for equality

**Where addressed:**
- Week 2 (Equal or Not) — foundational
- Week 3 (Body Math Halves) — reinforced via equal detection
- Crumb character throughout the year

**Resolution language:**
> *"Halves must be EQUAL. Any two pieces can be 'pieces.' Only EQUAL two pieces are HALVES."*

---

### M4 — Equal Means Identical

**The Misconception:** "Equal means the two pieces look exactly the same."

**Evidence:**
- Child rejects a half-circle and a different-oriented half-circle as "equal"
- Worries when pieces have same area but different shape

**Where addressed:**
- Week 2, Day 4 (Equal in Many Forms)
- Week 6 (Fee, Fi, Fo, Fum — different people, same fourths)

**Resolution language:**
> *"Equal means the SAME AMOUNT. It doesn't have to LOOK the same. Halfie and Halvo stand different ways but are equal halves."*

---

### M5 — More Pieces = Bigger Fraction

**The Misconception:** "If I have MORE pieces, each piece is bigger." (Or the reverse: "Fourth sounds bigger than half because 'four' is a bigger number.")

**Evidence:**
- Child believes fourths are bigger than halves because "4 > 2"
- Is confused when shown that more shares = smaller shares

**Where addressed:**
- Week 6 (Fourths Festival) — direct visual comparison
- Weeks 18-20 (Compositional work)
- Grade 2 continues this work with formal comparison

**Resolution language:**
> *"When you share with MORE friends, each friend gets LESS. Two friends = halves (bigger pieces). Four friends = fourths (smaller pieces). The MORE we share, the SMALLER each share."*

---

### M6 — Whole Disappears When Divided

**The Misconception:** "When I cut a whole into pieces, the whole is gone."

**Evidence:**
- Child struggles with "two halves make a whole" composition
- Treats fraction pieces as independent, unrelated objects

**Where addressed:**
- Weeks 26-27 (Build a Whole)
- Reunification narrative (Lord Notenough scatters wholes)

**Resolution language:**
> *"The whole doesn't disappear. The PIECES TOGETHER ARE STILL THE WHOLE. Two halves = one whole. Always."*

---

### M7 — Position Matters for Fraction Identity

**The Misconception:** "A half on top looks different from a half on the bottom, so they can't both be halves."

**Evidence:**
- Child rejects rotated halves as equal
- Confused by fractions drawn in non-standard orientations

**Where addressed:**
- Week 7 Day 3 (Rectangle folding variations)
- Week 12 (Recognition in varied pictures)

**Resolution language:**
> *"Halves can be turned any direction. A half turned sideways is STILL a half. The PIECE is what matters, not HOW it sits."*

---

### M8 — Every Whole Can Be Halved Into Whole Objects

**The Misconception:** "If I have 5 cookies, I can halve them and each person gets 2-and-a-half cookies." (Assuming children can halve individual objects.)

**Evidence:**
- Child doesn't recognize that halving SETS requires even numbers
- Or: child doesn't understand that physically halving a cookie is a separate act from halving a set

**Where addressed:**
- Week 9 (Set halving — explicit evenness discussion)
- Week 16 (Fractions in sets)

**Resolution language:**
> *"To halve a SET, we need an EVEN number. 6 cookies halve to 3 each. 8 cookies halve to 4 each. 5 cookies don't halve into whole cookies — we'd have to share the fifth one."*

---

### M9 — Fraction Vocabulary is Interchangeable with Cutting Vocabulary

**The Misconception:** "Halving means cutting. Any cutting is halving."

**Evidence:**
- Child uses "halve" for any cutting regardless of result
- Doesn't distinguish halving from other cuts

**Where addressed:**
- Weeks 11-14 (Equal Detectives — vocabulary precision)
- Lord Notenough's "vocabulary spies"

**Resolution language:**
> *"Halve means MAKE TWO EQUAL PARTS. Cutting just means using scissors. They are different! You can cut without halving."*

---

### M10 — Fourth and Quarter Are Different Things

**The Misconception:** "A fourth and a quarter are different — they must mean different fractions."

**Evidence:**
- Child treats "one-fourth" and "one-quarter" as distinct
- Confuses when teacher uses interchangeably

**Where addressed:**
- Week 11 (Vocabulary introduction, synonymy taught explicitly)
- Used deliberately interchangeably throughout Phase 2-3

**Resolution language:**
> *"Fourth and quarter mean THE SAME THING. Two words, one idea."*

---

### M11 — Fractions Only Exist in Food

**The Misconception:** "Fractions are a pizza thing. They don't apply to time, money, or other things."

**Evidence:**
- Child resists applying fraction language to non-food contexts
- Struggles with Phase 3 transfer tasks

**Where addressed:**
- Weeks 21-32 (Phase 3 — intentional transfer to time, money, length, sets)
- Home Hunt homework

**Resolution language:**
> *"Fractions are EVERYWHERE. Half an hour. Quarter past. Half a dollar. Fourth of a ribbon. Math is not just about food."*

---

### M12 — I'm Not a Math Person (Identity Misconception)

**The Misconception:** "I'm not good at math. This stuff is too hard. Other kids get it and I don't."

**Evidence:**
- Child expresses negative math identity
- Avoids math work
- Says "I can't"

**Where addressed:**
- Throughout the year via identity-safe pedagogy
- Character design (Pia makes mistakes; Crumb is confused but loved)
- Celebration of growth over achievement
- Explicit identity work in Week 32 (T7 objective)

**Resolution language:**
> *"In this kingdom, EVERYONE is a math person. Pia makes mistakes. Crumb gets confused. You might too. That's how we learn. You are NOT 'bad at math.' You are STILL LEARNING math — and you are GOOD AT LEARNING."*

## MISCONCEPTION DETECTION GUIDE

Teachers should watch for signs of each misconception throughout the year.

### Quick-Reference Detection:

| If you hear/see... | Suspect... | Action |
|---------------------|-----------|--------|
| "That's too small to be a whole" | M1 | Show varied-size wholes |
| "Only pizzas can be halved" | M2 | Introduce rectangle/square halving |
| "I halved it!" (pieces are unequal) | M3 | Revisit Equal Detective (Week 2) |
| "They're not the same shape" | M4 | Show different-shape equal pieces |
| "Four is bigger than two, so fourths are bigger" | M5 | Physical comparison: halve vs fourth same whole |
| Treats pieces as independent | M6 | Reunification work (Week 26-27) |
| Rejects rotated halves | M7 | Rotate examples during folding |
| "5 halved is 2-and-a-half each" | M8 | Discuss evenness in sets |
| Uses "halve" for any cut | M9 | Vocabulary precision work |
| Treats fourth and quarter as different | M10 | Teach synonymy explicitly |
| Won't apply fractions to time/money | M11 | Transfer tasks (Phase 3) |
| "I'm bad at math" | M12 | Identity-safe intervention |

## INTERVENTION PROTOCOLS

For persistent misconceptions (child shows same misconception for 2+ weeks despite instruction):

### Protocol A — Direct Confrontation
1. Observe the misconception in action
2. Provide a clear counter-example
3. Discuss the discrepancy: "You said X, but we see Y. What do you think?"
4. Let the child reason through the conflict
5. Re-practice with the new understanding

### Protocol B — Crumb Redirect
1. Show Crumb making the same mistake
2. Child corrects Crumb
3. Teacher notes: "You helped Crumb. Let's think if we ever do that too."
4. Lower-stakes self-reflection

### Protocol C — Peer Teaching
1. Pair child with a peer who has mastered this
2. Peer explains their understanding
3. Original child explains back
4. Teacher checks

### Protocol D — Family Involvement
For entrenched misconceptions, partner with family:
- Share what the misconception is (without shame)
- Give family a 5-min home activity to address it
- Follow up in 1 week

---

## ASSESSMENT OF MISCONCEPTIONS

Include misconception-detection in formative assessment:

- Provide non-examples alongside examples
- Ask "how do you know?" to surface reasoning
- Include trick questions (e.g., rotated halves, unequal pieces called "halves")

Responses reveal the child's current theory.

---

## CURRICULUM-LEVEL MISCONCEPTION AUDIT

Every 6 weeks, teachers should ask:
- Which misconceptions are most common in my class?
- Am I addressing them systematically?
- Which children need targeted support?
- Are my class activities inadvertently REINFORCING any misconceptions?
