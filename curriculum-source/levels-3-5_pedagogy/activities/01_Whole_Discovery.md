# ACTIVITY 01 — WHOLE DISCOVERY

> _"Before we can divide anything, we must know what one whole is. A fraction is meaningless without its whole."_

**Phase:** Discovery | **Week:** 1 | **Duration:** 5 days (30-45 min/day) | **Standard:** CCSS 1.G.A.3 (foundational)

---

## ACTIVITY OVERVIEW

**Goal:** Establish the concept of ONE WHOLE — a single, complete, undivided unit — before any partitioning occurs.

**Why This Activity First:**
Research (Behr et al., 1983; Empson & Levi, 2011) shows that the most common source of Grade 1 fraction failure is **weak whole-referent understanding**. Children who cannot clearly identify "the whole" will later struggle with: halves that are really fourths of a larger whole, comparing fractions across different wholes, and understanding that 1/2 of a small pizza ≠ 1/2 of a large pizza.

This activity is the **foundation pile** of the entire year. It is not optional preparation — it IS the first chunk of the curriculum.

**Learning Objective D1:**

> Students can identify a "whole" as a single, complete, undivided unit in multiple contexts (circle, rectangle, set, line) and articulate that the whole exists BEFORE it is divided.

---

## PEDAGOGICAL ANCHORS

- **Bruner's Concrete Stage:** All five days involve physical manipulatives.
- **Embodied Cognition:** Children hug, hold, and physically interact with wholes.
- **Pre-Requisite Logic:** No fraction language is used yet. We are not teaching "half" this week — we are teaching "one."
- **Narrative Integration:** Whola the Wise introduces herself and the Whole Hearth. Pia meets Whola for the first time.

---

## FIVE-DAY PLAN

### DAY 1 — MEET WHOLA AND THE WHOLE

**Duration:** 30 minutes

**Materials:**

- 1 large paper circle per child (12" diameter, uncut, undecorated)
- 1 read-aloud book: "Pia Meets Whola" (provided in `narrative/STORY_SCRIPTS.md`)
- Projector/smartboard (optional)

**Script (Teacher):**

> _"Class, today we meet someone new. Her name is Whola the Wise. Whola lives in the Halving Kingdom, and she is the keeper of the Whole Hearth. Let me read you her story."_

[Read "Pia Meets Whola" — 5 minutes]

> _"Whola taught Pia something very important. She said, 'Before we can share, we must know what ONE WHOLE is.' So today, we're going to learn what a WHOLE is."_

**Concrete Activity (20 minutes):**

1. Distribute one large paper circle to each child.
2. Say: _"This is your WHOLE. It is one. It is complete. It is not broken. Hold it up!"_
3. Children hold up their wholes.
4. Say: _"Hug your whole. Feel that it is ONE thing."_ (Embodied moment — children physically hold and connect with the whole.)
5. Say: _"Your whole is a pizza. It is ONE pizza. Not half a pizza. Not two pizzas. ONE pizza."_
6. Children decorate their pizza whole with crayons (toppings of their choice).
7. Say: _"Now I want you to walk your whole around the room. Show a friend. Say, 'THIS is my whole pizza.'"_
8. Children walk and share.
9. Gather. Ask: _"What is the same about everyone's pizza?"_ (Desired answer: they are all WHOLE, all one, all complete.)

**Closure:**

> _"Today we met Whola. Whola taught us that a WHOLE is one, complete thing. Tomorrow, we will find wholes everywhere."_

Children place their paper pizzas in a display area (the "Whole Hearth" in the classroom).

**Teacher Notes:**

- Do NOT introduce the words "half" or "fourth" this week.
- Do NOT cut or fold the wholes today.
- Listen for children who want to divide the pizza — gently redirect: "Not yet! Today we honor the whole."

---

### DAY 2 — WHOLES ARE EVERYWHERE

**Duration:** 35 minutes

**Materials:**

- Classroom objects (apple, book, cracker, pencil, shoe, crayon, block)
- Whole Hunt recording sheet (provided, `materials/01_Whole_Hunt_Sheet.pdf`)
- Crayons

**Opening (5 min):**

> _"Yesterday we made whole pizzas. But wholes aren't just pizzas. Wholes are everywhere. Can you find them?"_

Teacher holds up an apple.

> _"Is this a whole?"_
> Children: _"Yes!"_
> _"How do you know?"_
> Children: _"It's one! It's not cut! It's complete!"_

Teacher holds up a half-eaten cracker.

> _"Is THIS a whole?"_
> Children: _"No!"_
> _"Why not?"_
> Children: _"It's broken! It's not all there!"_

This is the critical distinction: **complete vs. incomplete**.

**Whole Hunt (20 min):**

Children receive recording sheets. They walk the classroom (in pairs) and identify 5 things that are WHOLE. They draw each one on the sheet and write (or dictate) what it is.

Teacher circulates, asking:

- _"Why is that a whole?"_
- _"What would make it not a whole?"_

**Gathering (10 min):**

Children share findings. Teacher records on chart paper:

- "Our Classroom Wholes: apple, chair, book, Maria's shoe, the window, the door, a crayon..."

Teacher introduces the word:

> _"All of these are WHOLES. They are ONE. Say it with me: WHOLE."_

**Closure:**

> _"We are becoming whole-finders. Tomorrow, we will learn that wholes don't have to be circles."_

---

### DAY 3 — DIFFERENT SHAPES, SAME WHOLE

**Duration:** 35 minutes

**Materials:**

- 5 large shapes per child (circle, square, rectangle, triangle, hexagon) cut from construction paper
- A "Whole Museum" display board
- Glue sticks

**Purpose:**
Combat the misconception that "whole = circle." Fractions must work on any shape. We lay this foundation early.

**Opening (10 min):**

Teacher holds up the circle.

> _"Yesterday we found many wholes. Is THIS a whole?"_ (circle) Children: _"YES!"_
> Teacher holds up the square.
> _"Is THIS a whole?"_
> Many children: _"Um..."_ — some hesitate.
> _"Let's think. Is it one? Is it complete? Is it unbroken?"_
> Children: _"Yes, yes, yes."_
> _"Then it IS a whole! A whole can be ANY shape!"_

**Activity (20 min):**

Each child receives 5 paper shapes. For each shape:

1. Hold it up.
2. Say aloud: _"This is a whole **\_\_** [name of shape]."_
3. Decorate it with a single drawing (a flower on the circle, a house on the rectangle, etc.)
4. Glue all 5 shapes onto a personal mat titled "My Wholes."

Teacher circulates and asks:

- _"Is your triangle a whole?"_
- _"How is your triangle-whole the same as your circle-whole?"_
- _"How is it different?"_

Desired outcome: children begin to articulate that "whole" is about COMPLETENESS, not shape.

**Whole Museum (5 min):**

Children post their mats on the "Whole Museum" display. Gallery walk: each child points to a classmate's whole and says "That's a whole **\_**."

**Closure:**

> _"Tomorrow we will meet Pia's friends, and they will have a big problem. Pia will need our help."_

---

### DAY 4 — THE SET AS A WHOLE

**Duration:** 40 minutes

**Materials:**

- 12 small counters per pair (pom-poms, bingo chips)
- 2 small paper plates per pair
- Story script: "Pia's First Market Day" (provided)

**Purpose:**
Introduce the concept that a WHOLE can be a SET of things (not just one object). A group of 4 cookies is a whole group. This is critical for later fraction-of-a-set work.

**Opening Story (10 min):**

Teacher reads:

> _"Pia went to the market to buy apples. She bought FOUR apples. She put them in one basket. The basket has four apples — but it is ONE whole basket of apples. Can a group be a whole?"_

Teacher holds up a plate with 4 pom-poms.

> _"THIS plate has 4 pom-poms. But the plate itself is ONE plate. The plate is a whole."_

**Activity (20 min):**

Pairs work with counters and plates:

1. Put 4 counters on one plate. Say: "This is ONE whole plate of counters."
2. Put 6 counters on one plate. Say: "This is STILL one whole plate of counters. The whole has more stuff now."
3. Put 2 counters on one plate. Say: "STILL one whole plate."

Teacher emphasizes: _"The WHOLE doesn't depend on how MANY things are on the plate. The WHOLE is the plate ITSELF."_

Then: 4. Put 4 counters on one plate, 4 counters on another plate. Ask: _"How many plates? How many wholes?"_ 5. Children answer: _"Two plates. Two wholes."_

This prepares for later work where "1/2 of one pizza" differs from "1/2 of two pizzas."

**Drawing Task (10 min):**

Children draw three "wholes" on a sheet:

- A whole pizza (single object)
- A whole box of 6 crayons (set of objects)
- A whole line of 5 ants (line/set)

They share drawings and explain why each is a whole.

**Closure:**

> _"Wholes can be ONE thing. Wholes can be a GROUP of things. Tomorrow, we bring everything together."_

---

### DAY 5 — THE WHOLE CELEBRATION AND ASSESSMENT

**Duration:** 45 minutes

**Materials:**

- "Wholes Portfolio" folder (each child has one)
- All work from Days 1-4
- Whole Celebration ceremony materials (optional: a "Whole Hearth" candle, soft music)
- Whole Assessment Sheet (provided, `assessment/01_Whole_Assessment.pdf`)

**Opening Ritual (10 min):**

Teacher dims lights slightly, lights a pretend candle (battery-operated). Plays a short Whola message (audio file or live reading):

> _"Dear young chefs. This week, you have honored the whole. You found wholes in pizzas, in shapes, in groups, in classrooms. You learned that a whole is ONE, COMPLETE, and UNBROKEN. Next week, you will learn to share your whole — but only because you know what your whole IS. I am proud of you. — Whola"_

Children applaud Whola.

**Portfolio Assembly (15 min):**

Each child receives their Wholes Portfolio folder. They add:

- Their decorated whole pizza (Day 1)
- Their Whole Hunt sheet (Day 2)
- Their shape wholes mat (Day 3)
- Their three-wholes drawing (Day 4)

They decorate the cover: "My Wholes, by **\_\_\_**"

**Assessment (15 min):**

Children complete the Whole Assessment Sheet individually:

1. Draw 3 different wholes.
2. Circle the wholes in a row of pictures (wholes and broken things mixed).
3. Explain to the teacher (1-on-1 conference): "What makes something a whole?"

**Teacher scoring rubric:**

- **Meeting:** Child identifies 3+ wholes, distinguishes whole from broken, articulates that a whole is "one, complete, not broken."
- **Developing:** Child identifies some wholes but conflates with size or shape.
- **Beginning:** Child cannot yet distinguish whole from broken; needs revisit.

**Closure (5 min):**

Teacher displays all portfolios in a "Whole Gallery." Class cheers.

> _"You are now WHOLE-KNOWERS. Next week, we meet Halfie and Halvo — and Pia will have her first big problem. Get ready."_

## DIFFERENTIATION

### For English Language Learners:

- Provide bilingual "whole" cards in Spanish (entero), Mandarin (整个), Arabic (كامل), and other classroom languages
- Use gestures consistently (hands forming a circle = "whole")
- Partner ELLs with a buddy for pair tasks

### For Students with Disabilities:

- Pre-cut shapes for students with fine-motor challenges
- Offer larger counters for students with dexterity needs
- Provide visual schedule showing each day's activity
- Adapted assessment with pointing/drawing options instead of verbal explanation

### For Advanced Students:

- Extension: "Can you find a whole that is NOT a shape?" (Time, sound, feeling) — stretches conceptual thinking
- Extension: "Is a sentence a whole? Is a song a whole?" (Abstract wholes)
- Journal prompt: "Explain to a kindergartener what a whole is."

### For Students Struggling:

- Extend to 2 weeks: more time with concrete objects
- 1-on-1 conferring with teacher: "Show me your whole. Tell me why it's a whole."
- Use larger, more obvious wholes (whole apple) before moving to abstract (whole set)

---

## COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS THIS WEEK

**M1: "A whole has to be a circle."**
_Mitigation:_ Day 3 explicitly uses many shapes.

**M2: "Bigger things are more whole than smaller things."**
_Mitigation:_ Children hold different-sized wholes (a whole grape vs. a whole pumpkin) and affirm both are wholes.

**M3: "A whole has to be one object."**
_Mitigation:_ Day 4 introduces wholes as sets.

**M4: "If I eat part of it, it's still a whole."**
_Mitigation:_ Day 2 explicitly contrasts whole (apple) with partial (half-eaten cracker).

**M5: "The word 'whole' is the same as 'hole.'"**
_Mitigation:_ Teacher explicitly addresses: "There are TWO words that sound alike. A HOLE is an empty space. A WHOLE is one complete thing. We write them differently."

Note: No fraction-specific misconceptions emerge yet, because we haven't introduced fractions.

## RESEARCH JUSTIFICATION

This week-long "Whole Discovery" activity exists because:

1. **Behr, Lesh, Post, & Silver (1983)** identified "unit construction" as the most fragile concept in early fraction learning. Students who confuse the whole with the piece fail catastrophically at magnitude judgments.

2. **Empson & Levi (2011)** in _Extending Children's Mathematics_ show that 30-60 minutes of isolated whole-referent work saves weeks of confusion later.

3. **Common Core Progressions Document (Fractions K-5)** explicitly positions whole-referent understanding as prerequisite, not concurrent, with partitioning.

4. **Lamon (2012)** shows that even Grade 3 students who struggle with fractions typically have unresolved whole-referent confusion traceable to Grades 1-2.

A week spent on "just wholes" is not slow. It is necessary.
