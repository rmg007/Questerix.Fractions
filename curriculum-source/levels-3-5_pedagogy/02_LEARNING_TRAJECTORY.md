# GRADE 1 LEARNING TRAJECTORY — THE HALVING KINGDOM JOURNEY

> *"A trajectory is not a schedule. It is a theory of how children learn. The trajectory tells us WHY this must come before that, not merely that it does."*

---

## PHILOSOPHICAL FRAMING

A **learning trajectory** (Clements & Sarama, 2014) is not a list of what children will do. It is a hypothesized path of cognitive development — a model of how one conceptual achievement builds on the one before it. This document articulates our theory for Grade 1 fractions.

Trajectories matter because:

- **Teachers need to know when to slow down.** If a child hasn't mastered the current level, accelerating does harm.
- **Assessments need to be grain-sized to the trajectory.** You cannot assess "fraction understanding" — you must assess a specific level of development.
- **Interventions need to target the right level.** A child struggling with Level 5 might need Level 3 work, not more Level 5.

This document provides the trajectory, week-by-week pacing, learning objectives, and the internal logic that links them.

---

## TRAJECTORY STRUCTURE

The Grade 1 fraction trajectory has three phases, eight levels, and twenty-three specific learning objectives.

### Phase 1: DISCOVERY (Weeks 1-6) — The Concrete Foundation

Children build the pre-mathematical intuitions that fractions will later name. No formal fraction vocabulary until Week 3.

- **Level 1:** Whole-referent understanding
- **Level 2:** Equality recognition
- **Level 3:** Halving through body and fold

### Phase 2: FORMALIZATION (Weeks 7-20) — The Named and Recognized

Children acquire the vocabulary, recognition skills, and compositional understanding of halves and fourths.

- **Level 4:** Halving across contexts
- **Level 5:** Fourthing as "half of a half"
- **Level 6:** Naming and recognition
- **Level 7:** Compositional reasoning (2 halves = whole, 4 fourths = whole)

### Phase 3: TRANSFER (Weeks 21-32) — The Applied and Generalized

Children apply halves and fourths in novel contexts and begin identity formation as fraction-users.

- **Level 8:** Transfer to real-world contexts

## LEVEL-BY-LEVEL TRAJECTORY

### LEVEL 1 — WHOLE-REFERENT UNDERSTANDING (Week 1)

**Cognitive Claim:**
Children cannot meaningfully divide what they do not first see as ONE. The whole-referent is the invisible but essential anchor for all fraction understanding.

**Objectives:**
- **D1:** Identify a "whole" as a single, complete, undivided unit across multiple contexts (single object, set of objects, line, region).
- **D1.1:** Distinguish "whole" from "partial" (half-eaten, broken, incomplete).
- **D1.2:** Articulate that a whole can be any shape, size, or context.

**Mastery Indicator:**
Given five pictures (three wholes, two partials), child correctly identifies all three wholes and explains why the others are not.

**Activity:** 01_Whole_Discovery

**Assessment:** Whole Portfolio + individual interview (assessment/01_Whole_Assessment.pdf)

**Prerequisite:** Counting to 10 (K standard); awareness of one-to-one correspondence.

**Risk If Skipped:** Children build fractions on a weak foundation. Later confusions about "half of what?" trace directly to Level 1 gaps.

---

### LEVEL 2 — EQUALITY RECOGNITION (Week 2)

**Cognitive Claim:**
"Half" is not "any two pieces" — it is "two equal pieces." Before children can produce equal pieces, they must perceive equality visually and articulate it verbally. Perception precedes production.

**Objectives:**
- **D2:** Distinguish equal from unequal visual pairs.
- **D3:** Articulate WHY two items are equal or unequal.
- **D4:** Use language: "equal," "same," "match," "fair," "more," "less."

**Mastery Indicator:**
Given 8 visual pairs (mix of equal and unequal, including tricky cases like same-amount-different-shape), child sorts correctly and explains at least 3 judgments.

**Activity:** 02_Equal_Or_Not

**Assessment:** Equality Assessment Sheet + conference (assessment/02_Equality_Assessment.pdf)

**Prerequisite:** Level 1; visual discrimination skills typical of age 6.

**Risk If Skipped:** Children will halve paper without recognizing that their halves are unequal. Teacher will "correct" the halves without the child understanding why.

---

### LEVEL 3 — HALVING THROUGH BODY AND FOLD (Week 3)

**Cognitive Claim:**
Halving is first a BODILY experience (the body's bilateral symmetry), then a GESTURE (the fold), then a CONCEPT ("one of two equal parts of a whole"). This ordering is non-negotiable for age 6.

**Objectives:**
- **D5:** Locate the "line of halving" (symmetry) in one's own body and in symmetric shapes.
- **D6:** Fold simple shapes so that two halves match.
- **D7:** Articulate that "half" = one of two equal parts of a whole.

**Mastery Indicator:**
Child folds a circle into halves, identifies the fold line, cuts into two parts, and (when asked) states: "This is half because it's one of two equal parts."

**Activity:** 03_Body_Math_Halves

**Assessment:** Fold Journal + Cutting Task + Explanation Interview (assessment/03_Halving_Assessment.pdf)

**Prerequisite:** Levels 1-2; fine motor for basic folding.

**Risk If Skipped:** Children learn "halving" as a disconnected motor task. They cannot generalize to non-folded contexts.

**Week 4 Integration:** Week 4's full-day Halving Adventure provides extended practice and depth at this level before moving on. See `activities/04_The_Halving_Adventure.md`.

---

### LEVEL 4 — HALVING ACROSS CONTEXTS (Weeks 5-6, 7-9)

**Cognitive Claim:**
A concept is not mastered until it transfers. Children who can halve a circle cannot necessarily halve a rectangle, a ribbon, a set of blocks, or a dough ball — each transfer is a learning event.

**Objectives:**
- **F1:** Halve a region (circle, square, rectangle, triangle, irregular shapes).
- **F2:** Halve a length (ribbon, line, string).
- **F3:** Halve a set of objects (count-based halving).
- **F4:** Recognize halves even when the two halves are different-looking (rotated, different orientations).

**Mastery Indicator:**
Given 6 different wholes (mix of regions, lengths, sets, and tricky rotations), child halves each one and verifies each is truly halved.

**Activities:** 05_Pizza_Party_Protocol, 07_Folding_Fun (partial coverage continues into Weeks 8-9)

**Assessment:** Halving-Across-Contexts Portfolio with 6 entries.

**Prerequisite:** Level 3.

**Note on Rotations:**
The challenge case — a circle cut horizontally vs. vertically vs. diagonally — is where many children FAIL. They see different-looking halves and conclude they are different fractions. This is a profound misconception requiring explicit attention. Queen Equallia's truth-water ritual reinforces: "These LOOK different but they are equal halves."

---

### LEVEL 5 — FOURTHING AS "HALF OF A HALF" (Weeks 5-10)

**Cognitive Claim:**
Children should discover fourths as "halving again" — NOT as "divide into four." The compositional move is pedagogically superior because it links fourths to halves naturally.

**Objectives:**
- **F5:** Create fourths by halving a half.
- **F6:** Identify fourths in multiple contexts (regions, lengths, sets).
- **F7:** Articulate: "A fourth is one of four equal parts. It's also half of a half."

**Mastery Indicator:**
Given a whole, child folds it into halves, then folds again to make fourths, and can explain the compositional relationship.

**Activity:** 06_The_Fourths_Festival

**Assessment:** Fourth-Discovery Portfolio + interview.

**Prerequisite:** Level 4 (reliable halving in multiple contexts).

**Why "Half of a Half" Matters:**
This framing is compositional. It teaches children that fractions are NESTED. This idea will serve them in Grades 2-5:
- A half IS two fourths
- A fourth IS half of a half
- Fractions can be decomposed and recomposed

Teaching fourths as "divide by 4" is atomic and blocks the compositional understanding that fraction operations later require.

---

### LEVEL 6 — NAMING AND RECOGNITION (Weeks 11-16)

**Cognitive Claim:**
Once children can DO halving and fourthing, they must acquire the LANGUAGE to name, describe, and reason about their actions. This level is mostly vocabulary and recognition practice, embedded in varied contexts.

**Objectives:**
- **F8:** Use the words "half," "halves," "fourth," "fourths," "quarter," "quarters" correctly.
- **F9:** Recognize halves and fourths in pictures and real-world contexts (clocks, rulers, pie charts in kid-form).
- **F10:** Name what fraction a piece represents ("this is one half," "this is one fourth").

**Mastery Indicator:**
Given 10 images (mix of halves, fourths, and neither), child correctly names each and uses the word "half" or "fourth" correctly in a sentence.

**Activities:** 08_Equal_Detectives, 09_Snack_Time_Math

**Assessment:** Naming Inventory + Use-in-Sentence Task.

**Prerequisite:** Level 5.

**Pedagogical Note:**
We explicitly do NOT teach symbolic notation (1/2, 1/4) in Grade 1. CCSS does not require it; research (Tzur, 2020) shows early symbolic introduction weakens magnitude understanding. Symbols arrive in Grade 2.

---

### LEVEL 7 — COMPOSITIONAL REASONING (Weeks 17-20)

**Cognitive Claim:**
The deepest Grade 1 concept is that fractions COMPOSE. Two halves together make a whole. Four fourths together make a whole. Two fourths together make a half. This is not just vocabulary — it is structural insight.

**Objectives:**
- **F11:** State and verify: "Two halves make a whole."
- **F12:** State and verify: "Four fourths make a whole."
- **F13:** State and verify: "Two fourths make one half."
- **F14:** Reassemble pieces to recover the whole.

**Mastery Indicator:**
Child can take a whole, cut it into halves, cut the halves into fourths, and reassemble back to the whole, articulating each compositional step.

**Activity:** 11_Build_A_Whole

**Assessment:** Decomposition/Composition Performance Task + oral reasoning.

**Prerequisite:** Level 6.

**The Critical Milestone:**
Act II Climax — the Halving Detective Trial (Week 20) — requires Level 7 mastery. Children who reach this level by Week 20 are on-trajectory for Grade 2.

---

### LEVEL 8 — TRANSFER TO REAL-WORLD CONTEXTS (Weeks 21-32)

**Cognitive Claim:**
Fractions learned in the Halving Kingdom must live OUTSIDE the kingdom. Transfer is the ultimate test of understanding.

**Objectives:**
- **T1:** Recognize and describe halves and fourths in food (pizza slices, sandwich halves, quartered apple).
- **T2:** Recognize and describe halves and fourths in time (half an hour, quarter past).
- **T3:** Recognize and describe halves and fourths in money (half a dollar, quarter).
- **T4:** Recognize and describe halves and fourths in length (half of a ribbon, quarter of a stick).
- **T5:** Recognize and describe halves and fourths in sets (half the crayons, fourth of the class).
- **T6:** Solve word problems involving halving and fourthing.
- **T7:** Identify themselves as "a fraction-knower" — identity formation.

**Mastery Indicator:**
Given a novel real-world problem (e.g., "Share 8 cookies with 4 friends — how many does each get?"), child reasons using fraction language and solves correctly.

**Activities:** 10_Story_Solver, 12_Real_World_Hunt, multiple extensions

**Assessment:** Transfer Portfolio with 7 domain entries + end-of-year performance task.

**Prerequisite:** Level 7.

## PACING FLEXIBILITY

This is a **34-week** curriculum designed for a typical 36-week school year. The two-week buffer is intentional:

- **Revisit weeks** for students who need more time at any level
- **Extension weeks** for advanced students
- **Holidays, testing, field trips** that disrupt flow
- **Teacher discretion** for what needs more time

**Pacing Flexibility Principle:**
If more than 30% of a class has not reached the mastery indicator for a level, the teacher should hold that level for another week rather than accelerate. Trajectory-aware teaching means letting the trajectory dictate pace, not the calendar.

## ASSESSMENT ALIGNMENT

Each level has:
- **A formative assessment** (during the week of instruction)
- **A portfolio piece** (collected and retained for year-end)
- **An observational rubric** (teacher records anecdotal data)
- **A re-teach trigger** (criterion for slowing down)

## DIFFERENTIATION ACROSS THE TRAJECTORY

Children do NOT advance uniformly. The trajectory provides:

- **Branch points:** Places where advanced students can extend while others consolidate
- **Re-entry points:** If a child falters at Level 6, they can re-enter at Level 4 without penalty
- **Parallel tracks:** Students with significant disabilities can work on modified objectives aligned to their IEP

See `differentiation/` folder for detailed branch plans.

## TRAJECTORY VS. STANDARDS

**CCSS 1.G.A.3** states:
> *"Partition circles and rectangles into two and four equal shares, describe the shares using the words halves, fourths, and quarters, and use the phrases half of, fourth of, and quarter of. Describe the whole as two of, or four of the shares. Understand for these examples that decomposing into more equal shares creates smaller shares."*

Our trajectory exceeds this standard by:
- Adding whole-referent understanding as a prerequisite (Level 1)
- Adding equality recognition (Level 2)
- Embedding halves/fourths in MANY contexts, not just circles/rectangles (Levels 4, 8)
- Adding compositional reasoning (Level 7)
- Adding transfer (Level 8)

This is **intentional rigor beyond the standard**. Grade 2 curriculum will thank us.
