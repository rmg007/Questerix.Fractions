# Pilot Protocol 2: Friction-Discovery Protocol (Round 1)

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-05-05  
**Plan:** Plan 15 (pilot-protocol), Phase 2  
**Scope:** Usability & pedagogical friction identification, L1–L3 focus  
**Participants:** 8–12 K–2 learners (1 per session, 30 min each)  
**Facilitator:** Single trained observer (no interruption unless child is stuck >2 min)  

---

## 1. Objectives

**Primary:**
- Identify **where and why** children hesitate, fail, or abandon tasks in L1–L3
- Categorize friction by type: **interaction** (can't figure out drag), **conceptual** (doesn't understand "equal"), **motivation** (loses interest)
- Validate that current partition/identify/label archetypes are learner-accessible without extensive scaffolding

**Secondary:**
- Note device-specific friction (touchscreen vs. mouse/trackpad)
- Identify common misconceptions triggering (e.g., children selecting "not equal" when parts *look* equal but have slight area error)
- Collect verbatim quotes for pedagogical clarity ("I thought the slices had to be the same COLOR")

**Decision gate (exit criteria):**
- **Green:** 8/10+ kids reach L3 without restart; avg hesitation time <10 sec per interaction
- **Yellow:** 5–7/10 kids succeed; avg hesitation 10–20 sec; 1–2 recurrent friction points identified
- **Red:** <5/10 succeed; avg hesitation >20 sec; 3+ distinct friction categories; recommend Phase 1 rework before scaling

---

## 2. Session Structure (30 min per child)

### Pre-Session (2 min)
1. **Setup:** Child sits at device, facilitator sits slightly behind and to the side (visible but not hovering)
2. **Assent refresh:** "Remember, you can stop anytime. I'm just watching and taking notes."
3. **No demo:** Do NOT show the child how to drag or solve a problem in advance

### Gameplay (20–22 min)
- **Child plays through L1 → L2 → L3** in sequence
- **Facilitator observes silently** unless:
  - Child is visibly stuck (no action for >2 min), then ask: "Would a hint help?"
  - Child asks a direct question (e.g., "How do I cut this?")
  - Device error or crash

### Post-Gameplay (6–8 min)
1. **Immediate debrief (no coaching):**
   - "What was the easiest level?"
   - "What was the hardest?"
   - "Did anything confuse you?"
2. **Device reflection (if applicable):**
   - "Was it easy to drag on [tablet/computer]?"
   - "Did your finger/mouse do what you wanted?"
3. **One-on-one misconception probe (optional):**
   - Show a partition task on paper: "Pretend I cut a rectangle into two pieces. Are they equal?" (show unequal pieces)
   - Listen to their reasoning without correcting

## 9. Decision Logic (Gate Criteria)

**GREEN (proceed to Phase 3):**
- L1–L3 combined success rate ≥85% (at least 10/12 kids per level)
- No more than 1 recurrent friction category per level
- No device-specific blockers (e.g., "touchscreen drag is unusable")
- Avg hesitation per interaction ≤8 sec

**YELLOW (Phase 1 tweaks, then retest):**
- L1–L3 success rate 70–84%
- 1–2 recurrent friction categories per level (e.g., "partition transition needs tighter visual feedback")
- Isolated device issue (e.g., trackpad drag; fixable)
- Avg hesitation 8–15 sec per interaction
- **Action:** Implement small UI/pedagogy fixes (visual feedback, instruction clarity) and retest with 4–6 new kids

**RED (Phase 1 redesign before scaling):**
- L1–L3 success rate <70%
- 3+ recurrent friction categories
- Systemic device blockers (e.g., "drag interaction doesn't work on touchscreen")
- Avg hesitation >15 sec per interaction; frequent resets or hint requests
- **Action:** Pause Round 2 expansion; conduct UX sprint to fix foundational issues; retest before scaling to L4–L9

---

## 10. Notes for Facilitators

1. **Be silent:** If the child doesn't ask, don't explain. Let them struggle a bit (up to 2 min).
2. **One hint only:** If they ask or get stuck, offer ONE hint, then let them try again.
3. **No coaching:** Don't say "Try dragging horizontally" or "That's a good partition idea." Stay neutral.
4. **Capture verbatim:** Write down exact words (especially misconception statements) for later analysis.
5. **Note emotion:** If a child seems frustrated or delighted, mark it — affects engagement judgment.
6. **Device neutral:** Don't blame the device if a child struggles; just note the friction and move on.
