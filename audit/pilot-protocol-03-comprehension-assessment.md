# Pilot Protocol 3: Comprehension-Assessment Protocol (Round 2)

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-05-05  
**Plan:** Plan 15 (pilot-protocol), Phase 3  
**Scope:** Pedagogical mastery validation, L1–L6 focus; measures transfer and misconception resistance  
**Participants:** 10–15 K–2 learners (1 per session, 40 min each)  
**Dependency:** Requires GREEN or YELLOW gate from Protocol 2 (Round 1 friction discovery)  
**When:** After Phase 1 UX fixes (if YELLOW) or Phase 2 content expansion (if GREEN)

---

## 1. Objectives

**Primary:**
- Validate that children who **complete L1–L6 demonstrate fraction mastery** (can identify, partition, compare at grade-level)
- Measure **transfer:** Can they apply partition skills to identify tasks? Can they use benchmarks (L8) without explicit instruction?
- Detect **misconception persistence:** Do children still exhibit whole-is-biggest bias (MC-WHB-02) or part-counting errors (MC-EOL-01) after gameplay?

**Secondary:**
- Quantify learning gain: Compare pre-test (before first level) to post-test (after L6)
- Identify **bottleneck transitions:** L2→L3 (partition → equal-or-not), L3→L4 (identifying → making), L5→L6 (snap_match → compare)
- Gather evidence of conceptual understanding vs. procedural fluency (e.g., child clicks correct answer but can't explain WHY)

**Decision gate (exit criteria):**
- **Green:** ≥75% post-test accuracy on L6-level tasks; <20% misconception persistence; smooth progression through all 6 levels
- **Yellow:** 60–74% accuracy; 20–35% misconception; 1 bottleneck transition slowing progress
- **Red:** <60% accuracy; >35% misconception; 2+ bottleneck transitions or evidence that content doesn't transfer

---

## 2. Session Structure (40 min per child)

### Pre-Test (5 min)
1. **Baseline fraction readiness (no gameplay prep):**
   - Show printed fraction cards (1/2, 1/4, 2/3)
   - Ask: "Do you know what this means?" (record yes/no + explanation)
   - No scoring; just learning what child brings to the table

### Gameplay (25 min)
- **Child plays L1 → L2 → L3 → L4 → L5 → L6 in sequence**
- Facilitator **observes only** (same as Protocol 2)
- Track time per level and hint requests

### Post-Test (8–10 min)
- **Comprehension questions** (asked verbally, no gameplay)
- **Visual transfer tasks** (paper-and-pencil, not on screen)
- **Misconception probes**

**Key transitions to monitor:**
- **L2→L3 friction:** Does child understand that equal parts don't have to look like the exact same shape (e.g., two right triangles from cutting a rectangle diagonally are still equal in area)?
- **L3→L4 friction:** Does child treat "make" as a new concept, or apply "equal parts" logic from prior levels?
- **L5→L6 friction:** Does child use the symbolic fraction (1/2) learned in L5 to reason about comparisons in L6, or does L6 feel like a new task?

**Transfer evidence checklist:**
- [ ] Child spontaneously applies prior strategy to new context
- [ ] Child says something like "Oh, like what we did before…"
- [ ] Child explicitly connects two levels ("This is like the equal-or-not, but now I'm picking which one…")
- [ ] No transfer observed; child treats each level as isolated

## 5. Post-Test: Comprehension Questions (5 min)

**Facilitator asks verbally, child responds without seeing the screen.**

### Set A: Concept Verification (ask after levels complete)

**Q1: Equal parts understanding**
- **Prompt:** "Show me two pieces of paper. Cut one in half. Cut the other in half differently (e.g., diagonal vs. horizontal). Are these halves equal?"
- **Correct response:** "Yes, they're both halves because they have the same area / take up the same amount" OR "The pieces are the same size, just different shapes"
- **Score:** [ ] Correct (area reasoning)  [ ] Partially correct (size but not articulate)  [ ] Incorrect (says "no" or "they don't look the same")

**Q2: Fraction identification transfer**
- **Prompt:** "If I show you a circle and color in this much [point to 3/4 of circle], what fraction is that?"
- **Correct response:** "Three fourths" or "3/4"
- **Score:** [ ] Correct  [ ] Incorrect

**Q3: Comparison reasoning**
- **Prompt:** "Is 1/2 bigger than 1/4? How do you know?"
- **Correct response:** "Yes, because halves are bigger pieces than fourths" OR "I can see 1/2 is more on the bar"
- **Score:** [ ] Correct with reasoning  [ ] Correct without reasoning  [ ] Incorrect

---

### Set B: Visual Transfer Tasks (paper, no screen)

**Task B1: Free partition (no scaffolding)**
- **Prompt:** "Draw a rectangle on this paper. Cut it into thirds (three equal parts). Show me where you'd cut."
- **Score:**
  - [ ] Correct (three equal parts)
  - [ ] Partially correct (three unequal parts; shows effort)
  - [ ] Incorrect (doesn't partition or cuts unequal parts)
- **Follow-up:** "How did you decide where to cut?"

**Task B2: Identify and label**
- **Prompt:** "I'm going to show you three pictures. One shows 2/4 (two fourths). Which one?" (show three visual options on paper, same as L2 identify task)
  - Option A: 4-part rectangle, 2 parts shaded ✓
  - Option B: 4-part rectangle, 1 part shaded
  - Option C: 3-part rectangle, 2 parts shaded
- **Score:** [ ] Correct  [ ] Incorrect (which distractor?)

**Task B3: Compare without number line**
- **Prompt:** "Which is bigger: 3/4 or 1/4?" (show two bar pictures on paper)
- **Score:** [ ] Correct  [ ] Incorrect
- **Follow-up:** "How did you decide?"

## 6. Misconception Probe (3 min)

**Use same probes as Protocol 2, plus new ones specific to L4–L6:**

| Misconception ID | Probe Task | Child Response | Category |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MC-WHB-01** (whole-is-biggest) | Show a rectangle split in half horizontally and another split in half vertically. "Are these equal halves?" | [ ] Yes (correct)  [ ] No (misconception) | Geometric reasoning |
| **MC-EOL-01** (end-of-list bias) | "Which picture shows 2/3?" (present three options; correct is in the middle, distractor in position 0 is "count the shaded parts only") | [ ] Correct answer  [ ] Distractor (EOL bias)  [ ] Other | Selection bias |
| **MC-WHB-02** (denominator bigger = fraction bigger) | Show 1/8 and 1/4 on bar. "Which one is bigger?" | [ ] Correct (1/4)  [ ] Incorrect; says 1/8 (misconception) | Whole-is-biggest reasoning |
| **MC-PRX-01** (visual proximity: "pieces touch, so equal") | Show 2 unequal pieces in a partition (one piece barely touches the edge but has less area). "Are these equal?" | [ ] Correct (no)  [ ] Incorrect; says yes because they touch | Spatial proximity |

**Pattern analysis:** If child shows same misconception across multiple probes, escalate for Phase 3 design feedback.

## 10. Decision Logic (Gate Criteria)

**GREEN (proceed to Phase 4 retention testing and L7–L9 expansion):**
- Post-test accuracy ≥75% across L1–L6
- Learning gain average ≥2/3 (pre-test → post-test)
- Misconception persistence <20% (children not repeating same errors in probes)
- No more than 1 bottleneck transition per level (all transfers natural or scaffolded)
- Mastery/Proficient level for ≥80% of sample

**YELLOW (targeted remediation, then retest):**
- Post-test accuracy 60–74%
- Learning gain 1–2/3
- Misconception persistence 20–35%
- 1–2 bottleneck transitions (e.g., L3→L4, L5→L6)
- Mastery/Proficient for 60–79% of sample
- **Action:** Identify specific transition friction; add scaffolding (hint ladder, visual feedback) and retest with 5–7 kids on bottleneck levels

**RED (redesign content, pause L7–L9 expansion):**
- Post-test accuracy <60%
- Learning gain <1/3
- Misconception persistence >35%
- 3+ bottleneck transitions
- Mastery/Proficient for <60% of sample
- **Action:** Conduct content audit; identify misconception drivers (e.g., visual feedback too subtle, transition scaffolding missing); redesign L1–L6 before scaling to later levels

---

## 11. Facilitator Notes

1. **Ask but don't lead:** "How did you decide?" is neutral; "You should compare the areas" is coaching.
2. **Probe misconceptions gently:** "Can you tell me how you figured that out?" opens reasoning; "That's wrong" closes it.
3. **Transfer is the goal:** If a child applies a strategy from one level to another unprompted, note it as high evidence of understanding.
4. **Timing matters:** If a child is rushed (e.g., 40 min session but took 35 min to play L1–L6), post-test may be less reliable; note the time pressure.
